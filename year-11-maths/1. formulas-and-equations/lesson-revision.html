Section 1

Substitution

Q1 2007 HSC ↗ 1 mark

The distance in kilometres (d) of an observer from the centre of a thunderstorm can be estimated by counting the number of seconds (n) between seeing the lightning and first hearing the thunder.

d = n ÷ 3

Use this formula to estimate the number of seconds between seeing the lightning and hearing the thunder if the storm is 1.2 km away.

Show solution
1.2 = n ÷ 3 n = 1.2 × 3 ∴ n = 3.6 seconds
Q2 2005 HSC ↗ 2 marks

The formula below is used to convert temperatures between degrees Fahrenheit (F) and degrees Celsius (C).

F = (9/5)C + 32

Convert 3°C to the equivalent temperature in Fahrenheit.

Show solution
F = (9/5)(3) + 32 F = 5.4 + 32 ∴ F = 37.4°F
Q3 2013 HSC ↗ 2 marks

For adults (18 years and older), the Body Mass Index is given by:

BMI = weight ÷ height²

where weight is in kilograms and height is in metres. The medically accepted healthy range for BMI is 18.5 ≤ BMI ≤ 25.

What is the minimum weight for a 163 cm adult female to be considered healthy?

Show solution
Height = 1.63 m Minimum BMI = 18.5 Minimum weight = 18.5 × (1.63)² = 18.5 × 2.6569 ≈ 49.2 kg

Section 2

Blood Alcohol Content

Q4 SM-Bank 1 mark

The blood alcohol content (BAC) of a male's blood is given by:

BAC(M) = (10N − 7.5H) ÷ (6.8M) where N = standard drinks consumed H = hours drinking M = person's mass (kg)

Calculate the BAC of a male who consumed 4 standard drinks in 3.5 hours and weighs 68 kg, correct to 2 decimal places.

  • A. 1.03
  • B. 0.03
  • C. 0.04
  • D. 0.01
Show solution
BAC = (10×4 − 7.5×3.5) ÷ (6.8×68) = (40 − 26.25) ÷ 462.4 = 13.75 ÷ 462.4 ≈ 0.03 → Answer: B
Q5 2016 HSC ↗ 1 mark

Caroline drinks two small bottles of wine over a three-hour period. Each of these bottles contains 2.3 standard drinks. Caroline weighs 53 kg. Using the formula below, what is Caroline's approximate blood alcohol content (BAC) at the end of this period?

BAC(F) = (10N − 7.5H) ÷ (5.5M) where N = standard drinks, H = hours, M = mass (kg)
  • A. 0.050
  • B. 0.054
  • C. 0.064
  • D. 0.069
Show solution
N = 2 × 2.3 = 4.6 standard drinks BAC = (10×4.6 − 7.5×3) ÷ (5.5×53) = (46 − 22.5) ÷ 291.5 = 23.5 ÷ 291.5 ≈ 0.081 — Note: check answer choices match the year's version of the formula. Work shown is the method.
Q6 2020 HSC ↗ 1 mark

When Jake stops drinking alcohol at 10:30 pm, he has a blood alcohol content (BAC) of 0.08375. The number of hours required for a person to reach zero BAC after they stop consuming alcohol is given by:

Time = BAC ÷ 0.015

At what time on the next day should Jake expect his BAC to be 0.05?

  • A. 12:45 am
  • B. 1:50 am
  • C. 2:15 am
  • D. 4:05 am
Show solution

Find the time for BAC to fall from 0.08375 to 0.05:

BAC drop needed = 0.08375 − 0.05 = 0.03375 Time = 0.03375 ÷ 0.015 = 2.25 hours 10:30 pm + 2 hours 15 min = 12:45 am Answer: A
Q7 2023 HSC ↗ 4 marks

The following formula can be used to calculate an estimate for blood alcohol content (BAC) for males:

BAC(M) = (10N − 7.5H) ÷ (6.8M) where N = standard drinks, H = hours, M = mass (kg)

Cameron weighs 75 kg. His BAC was zero when he began drinking alcohol. At 9:00 pm, after consuming 3 standard drinks, his BAC was 0.02. Using the formula, estimate at what time Cameron began drinking alcohol, to the nearest minute.

Show solution
0.02 = (10×3 − 7.5×H) ÷ (6.8×75) 0.02 = (30 − 7.5H) ÷ 510 0.02 × 510 = 30 − 7.5H 10.2 = 30 − 7.5H 7.5H = 19.8 H = 2.64 hours = 2 hours 38 minutes (0.64 × 60 ≈ 38 min) 9:00 pm − 2 h 38 min = 6:22 pm Cameron began drinking at approximately 6:22 pm.

Section 3

Medication Dosages

Q8 2017 HSC ↗ 1 mark

Young's formula is used to calculate the dosage of medication for children aged 1–12 years based on the adult dosage:

D = A ÷ (A + 12) × adult dose

where D = child dosage, A = age of child in years.

A child's dosage is calculated to be 20 mg, based on an adult dosage of 40 mg. How old is the child in years?

  • A. 3
  • B. 6
  • C. 9
  • D. 12
Show solution
20 = A ÷ (A + 12) × 40 A ÷ (A + 12) = 20 ÷ 40 = 0.5 A = 0.5(A + 12) = 0.5A + 6 0.5A = 6 → A = 12 Answer: D (12 years)
Q9 SM-Bank 2 marks

Fried's formula is used to calculate the medicine dosages for children aged 1–2 years:

D = (age in months ÷ 150) × adult dose

Ben is 1.5 years old and receives a daily dosage of 450 mg of a medicine. According to Fried's formula, what would the appropriate adult daily dosage of the medicine be?

Show solution
Age = 1.5 years = 18 months 450 = (18 ÷ 150) × adult dose 450 = 0.12 × adult dose Adult dose = 450 ÷ 0.12 = 3750 mg
Q10 2018 HSC ↗ 2 marks

Clark's formula is used to determine the dosage of medicine for children:

D = (child's weight × adult dose) ÷ 70

For a particular medicine, the adult dosage is 325 mg and the correct dosage for a specific child is 90 mg. How much does the child weigh, to the nearest kg?

Show solution
90 = (weight × 325) ÷ 70 weight × 325 = 90 × 70 = 6300 weight = 6300 ÷ 325 ≈ 19 kg
Q11 2015 HSC ↗ 2 marks

Clark's formula is used to determine the dosage of medicine for children:

D = (child's weight × adult dose) ÷ 70

The adult daily dosage of a medicine contains 3150 mg of a particular drug. A child who weighs 35 kg is to be given tablets each containing 525 mg of this drug. How many tablets should this child be given daily?

Show solution
Child's dose = (35 × 3150) ÷ 70 = 1575 mg Number of tablets = 1575 ÷ 525 = 3 tablets

Section 4

Speed, Distance & Time

Q12 2020 HSC ↗ 1 mark

The distance between Bricktown and Koala Creek is 75 km. A person travels from Bricktown to Koala Creek at an average speed of 50 km/h. How long does it take the person to complete the journey?

  • A. 40 minutes
  • B. 1 hour 25 minutes
  • C. 1 hour 30 minutes
  • D. 1 hour 50 minutes
Show solution
Time = Distance ÷ Speed = 75 ÷ 50 = 1.5 hours = 1 hour 30 minutes → Answer: C
Q13 2012 HSC ↗ 1 mark

A car takes 6 hours to complete a journey when travelling at 60 km/h. How long would the same journey take if the car were travelling at 100 km/h?

  • A. 36 minutes
  • B. 1 hour and 40 minutes
  • C. 3 hours and 6 minutes
  • D. 3 hours and 36 minutes
Show solution
Distance = 60 × 6 = 360 km Time = 360 ÷ 100 = 3.6 hours = 3 hours 36 minutes → Answer: D
Q14 SM-Bank 1 mark

Fleur lives 15 kilometres from her work. On Wednesday, she drove to work and averaged 60 kilometres per hour. On Thursday, she took the bus which averaged 15 kilometres per hour. What was the extra time of the bus journey, in minutes, compared to when she drove on Wednesday?

  • A. 15
  • B. 45
  • C. 60
  • D. 75
Show solution
Drive time = 15 ÷ 60 hours = 15 minutes Bus time = 15 ÷ 15 hours = 60 minutes Extra time = 60 − 15 = 45 minutes Answer: B

Section 5

Stopping Distance

Q15 2018 HSC ↗ 3 marks

Sophie is driving at 70 km/h. She notices a branch on the road ahead and decides to apply the brakes. Her reaction time is 1.5 seconds. Her braking distance (d metres) is given by:

d = v² ÷ 170 (where v is speed in km/h)

Stopping distance = reaction distance + braking distance. What is Sophie's stopping distance, to the nearest metre?

Show solution
Reaction distance = (70 ÷ 3.6) × 1.5 = 19.44 × 1.5 ≈ 29.2 m Braking distance = 70² ÷ 170 = 4900 ÷ 170 ≈ 28.8 m Stopping distance ≈ 29.2 + 28.8 ≈ 58 m
Q16 SM-Bank 2 marks

Doris is driving in a school zone at a speed of 35 km/h and needs to stop immediately to avoid an accident. It takes her 1.25 seconds to react and her braking distance is 5.3 metres. Stopping distance = reaction distance + braking distance.

What is Doris' total stopping distance? Give your answer to 1 decimal place.

Show solution
Reaction distance = (35 ÷ 3.6) × 1.25 = 9.722 × 1.25 ≈ 12.2 m Braking distance = 5.3 m Stopping distance = 12.2 + 5.3 = 17.5 m

Section 6

Linear Applications

Q17 2017 HSC ↗ 1 mark

Kate is comparing two different models of car. Car A uses fuel at the rate of 9 L/100 km. Car B uses 3.5 L/100 km. Suppose Kate plans on driving 8000 km in the next year. How much less fuel will she use driving Car B instead of Car A?

  • A. 44 L
  • B. 280 L
  • C. 440 L
  • D. 720 L
Show solution
Car A fuel = 9/100 × 8000 = 720 L Car B fuel = 3.5/100 × 8000 = 280 L Difference = 720 − 280 = 440 L → Answer: C
Q18 2021 HSC ↗ 2 marks

The fuel consumption for a car is 6.7 litres/100 km. On a road trip, the car travels a distance of 1560 km and the fuel cost is $1.45 per litre. What is the total fuel cost for the trip?

Show solution
Fuel used = 6.7/100 × 1560 = 104.52 L Total cost = 104.52 × $1.45 = $151.55
Q19 2022 HSC ↗ 3 marks

Tom is 25 years old and likes to keep fit by exercising.

(a) Use this formula to find his maximum heart rate (bpm):

Maximum heart rate = 220 − age in years

(b) Tom will get the most benefit from exercise if his heart rate is between 50% and 85% of his maximum heart rate. Between what two heart rates should Tom be aiming for?

Show solution
(a) Maximum heart rate = 220 − 25 = 195 bpm (b) Lower = 50% × 195 = 97.5 ≈ 98 bpm Upper = 85% × 195 = 165.75 ≈ 166 bpm Tom should aim for between 98 bpm and 166 bpm.
Q20 SM-Bank 2 marks

The weight of a steel beam (W kg) varies directly with its length (l mm). A 1200 mm steel beam weighs 144 kg. Calculate the weight of a 750 mm steel beam.

Show solution
W = kl → 144 = k × 1200 → k = 0.12 W = 0.12 × 750 = 90 kg
