Section 1

The Statistical Investigation Process

A statistical investigation follows a cycle of four key stages.

1Pose a question

Identify an issue and define what you want to find out.

2Collect data

Design a survey or study that will produce useful information.

3Analyse data

Organise, display, and summarise what was collected.

4Interpret and communicate

Draw conclusions and answer the original question.

Section 2

Posing and Designing Questions

What makes a good statistical question?

  • It targets a specific population.
  • It can be answered with data.
  • It expects variation in the responses.

Example

Issue: Screen time among teenagers.

Poor question: “Do you use your phone a lot?”

Better question: “How many hours per day do Year 11 students at this school spend on social media?”

Questionnaire design principles

  • Clear — plain language that everyone understands.
  • Unambiguous — only one possible interpretation.
  • Neutral — does not push the respondent toward an answer.
  • Appropriately scaled — the number of response choices fits the task.
  • Sensitive — respectful of diverse groups and backgrounds.

Clear vs leading

Ambiguous: “How often do you exercise?”

Better: “How many times per week do you participate in physical activity for at least 30 minutes?”

Leading: “Don’t you agree that homework should be banned?”

Better: “What is your opinion on homework in secondary schools?”

Type Example Issue
Too few choices Yes / No Forces oversimplification
Too many choices Scale of 1–100 Hard to answer consistently
Well-balanced Scale of 1–5 Captures a range of opinion
No neutral option Agree / Disagree only Forces a stance

Privacy, bias, ethics and cultural responsiveness

Privacy: respondents have the right to remain anonymous, especially for sensitive questions such as income, health, or religion.

Bias: a systematic error that skews results. It can come from wording, sample selection, or the way data is collected.

Ethics: participation should be voluntary, informed, and free from harm. Data must be reported honestly.

Cultural responsiveness: questions must be respectful and relevant to people from all backgrounds.

Examples of bias in surveys

Wording bias: “Most experts agree smoking is harmful. Do you support a smoking ban?”

Privacy concern: asking employees to rate their manager by name in a non-anonymous survey may produce dishonest answers.

Check yourself

If a question sounds emotional, vague, or loaded, rewrite it before the survey goes out.

Section 3

Population, Sample, and Census

Key terms

Population: the entire group that the investigation is about.

Sample: a subset of the population selected to represent the whole group.

Census: data collected from every member of the population.

A representative sample reflects the characteristics of the population. If the sample is not representative, the conclusions may be invalid.

Section 4

Sampling Methods

Method Description
Random sampling Every member of the population has an equal chance of selection.
Systematic sampling Select every nth member from an ordered list.
Stratified sampling Divide the population into strata and sample from each in proportion.
Self-selected sampling Participants volunteer themselves, as in an online poll.

Random sampling

Assign each student a number and use a random number generator to select the sample.

Systematic sampling

Choose every nth item from a list after a random starting point.

n = population size / sample size

Stratified sampling

Split the population into subgroups and sample each subgroup in proportion.

Self-selected sampling

Useful for quick polls, but strongly affected by voluntary response bias.

Method Representative? Main risk
Random Yes, if done correctly Requires a full sampling frame
Systematic Usually Pattern bias in the list
Stratified Yes More complex to organise
Self-selected Rarely Voluntary response bias

Section 5

Faults in Data Collection and Interpretation

Data collection methods

Survey: participants answer questions about themselves.

Experiment: the researcher controls a variable and measures the effect.

Observational study: the researcher observes and records without interference.

Common survey problems

  • Leading questions
  • Double-barrelled questions
  • Ambiguous language
  • Sensitive questions without anonymity
  • Response bias

Survey design fault

“Do you eat healthily and exercise regularly?” asks two separate questions and should be split into two items.

Experiment / observational faults

Confounding variables, observer effects, small sample sizes, and non-response bias can all distort results.

Misunderstandings and misrepresentations

  • Cherry-picking only the data that supports a desired conclusion.
  • Misleading graphs with truncated axes, inconsistent scales, or 3D effects.
  • Confusing correlation with causation.
  • Overgeneralising from a small or biased sample.

Example: a graph that starts at 900 instead of 0 can make a small change look dramatic.
