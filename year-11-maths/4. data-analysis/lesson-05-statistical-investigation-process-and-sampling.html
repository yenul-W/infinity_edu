Statistical Investigation Process and Sampling

Learn how to design rigorous statistical investigations and select representative samples.

Key takeaways

Statistical investigations follow a four-stage cycle.

Good surveys are clear, unambiguous, and neutral.

Representative samples are essential for valid conclusions.

Section 1

The Statistical Investigation Process

A statistical investigation follows a cycle of four key stages.

Stage 1: Pose a Question

Identify an issue and define what you want to find out.

  • Targets a specific population (the group you want information about)
  • Can be answered by collecting data
  • Anticipates variability in responses

Stage 2: Collect Data

Design a survey or study that will produce useful information.

  • Consider your sampling method
  • Ensure questions are well-designed
  • Collect data systematically

Stage 3: Analyse Data

Organise, display and summarise what was collected.

  • Create tables and graphs
  • Calculate summary statistics
  • Look for patterns

Stage 4: Interpret and Communicate

Draw conclusions and answer the original question.

  • Relate findings back to the question
  • Consider limitations of the data
  • Communicate results clearly
Example

Issue: Screen time among teenagers.

Poor question: "Do you use your phone a lot?"

Better question: "How many hours per day do Year 11 students at this school spend on social media?"

Section 2

Survey Design

Questionnaire Design Principles

A well-designed survey question must be:

  • Clear — written in plain language that all respondents understand
  • Unambiguous — only one possible interpretation
  • Neutral — does not lead the respondent toward a particular answer
  • Appropriately scaled — the number of response choices is suitable
  • Sensitive — respectful of diverse groups, cultures and backgrounds
Clear vs Ambiguous

Ambiguous: "How often do you exercise?"

"Often" means different things to different people.

Better: "How many times per week do you participate in physical activity for at least 30 minutes?"

  • Never • 1–2 times • 3–4 times • 5 or more times
Neutral vs Leading

Leading: "Don't you agree that homework should be banned?"

The phrasing pushes the respondent toward "yes".

Better: "What is your opinion on homework in secondary schools?"

Number of Response Choices

Type Example Issue
Too few choices Yes / No Forces oversimplification
Too many choices Scale of 1–100 Hard to answer consistently
Well-balanced Scale of 1–5 Captures range of opinion
No neutral option Agree / Disagree only Forces a stance

Tip: A 4 or 5 point scale is typical for opinion questions.

Privacy, Bias, Ethics and Cultural Responsiveness

Privacy: Respondents have the right to remain anonymous. Sensitive questions (income, health, religion) must be handled carefully.

Bias: A systematic error that skews results. Can arise from question wording, sample selection or the way data is collected.

Ethics: Participation should be voluntary, informed and free from harm. Data must be reported honestly.

Cultural responsiveness: Questions must be respectful and relevant to people from all backgrounds. Avoid assumptions based on culture, gender or identity.

Examples of Bias in Surveys

Wording bias: "Most experts agree smoking is harmful. Do you support a smoking ban?" — The phrasing pressures respondents to agree.

Privacy concern: Asking employees to rate their manager by name in a non-anonymous survey may cause them to give dishonest positive responses out of fear.

Section 3

Population and Sample

Key Terms

Population: The entire group that the investigation is about.

Sample: A subset of the population selected to represent the whole group.

Census: Data collected from every member of the population.

A representative sample reflects the characteristics of the population. If a sample is not representative, conclusions drawn from it may be invalid.

Section 4

Sampling Methods

Method Description
Random sampling Every member of the population has an equal chance of selection
Systematic sampling Select every nth member from an ordered list
Stratified sampling Divide the population into strata (subgroups) and sample from each in proportion
Self-selected sampling Participants volunteer themselves, as in an online poll
Random Sampling

Every member of the population has an equal chance of being selected. Assign each student a number and use a random number generator to select the sample. Minimises selection bias but requires a complete list (sampling frame).

Systematic Sampling

Select every nth member from an ordered list, starting from a randomly chosen point.

n = population size ÷ sample size

Example: A factory produces 1,000 items per day. To check quality, inspect every 20th item off the production line (items 20, 40, 60, …).

Stratified Sampling

The population is divided into subgroups (strata) based on a characteristic (e.g. year group, gender). Members are sampled from each stratum in proportion.

Example: A school of 600 students (200 in Year 10, 200 in Year 11, 200 in Year 12). To take a stratified sample of 60: From each year group = (60 ÷ 600) × 200 = 20 students

Self-Selected Sampling

Participants choose to take part themselves. Easy and inexpensive, but highly prone to voluntary response bias — those with strong opinions are more likely to respond. Example: An online poll asking "Should the school canteen serve healthier food?" — only students who feel strongly will bother to respond.

Comparing Sampling Methods

Method Representative? Main Risk
Random Yes (if done correctly) Requires full sampling frame
Systematic Usually Pattern bias in the list
Stratified Yes (best method) More complex to organise
Self-selected Rarely Voluntary response bias

Section 5

Faults in Data Collection and Interpretation

Types of Data Collection

Survey: Participants answer questions about themselves.

Experiment: The researcher controls a variable and measures the effect.

Observational study: The researcher observes and records without interference.

Faults in Survey Design

Common problems with surveys include:

  • Leading questions — wording that pushes respondents toward a particular answer
  • Double-barrelled questions — asking two things in one question
  • Ambiguous language — words that different people interpret differently
  • Sensitive questions without anonymity — causes dishonest responses
  • Response bias — participants answer in a socially desirable way rather than honestly

Example: "Do you eat healthily and exercise regularly?" asks two separate questions — it should be split into two distinct items.

Faults in Experiments and Observational Studies

  • Confounding variables — a third variable affects the result, making it unclear what caused the outcome
  • Observer effect — participants behave differently when they know they are being watched
  • Small sample size — results may not generalise to the population
  • Non-response bias — certain types of people consistently decline to participate, skewing the sample

Example: A study finds students who eat breakfast score higher on tests. But students from higher-income families may both eat breakfast and have better resources for study — income is a confounding variable.

Misunderstandings and Misrepresentations

Even after data is collected, results can be misrepresented:

  • Cherry-picking — reporting only the data that supports a desired conclusion
  • Misleading graphs — truncated axes, inconsistent scales, or 3D effects that distort proportions
  • Correlation vs causation — two variables may be related without one causing the other
  • Overgeneralisation — applying conclusions from a small or biased sample to the entire population

Example: A graph showing sales "up 50%" with a y-axis starting at 900 instead of 0 makes a small change look dramatic.
